One of the most adopted accounting systems for SMEs is Tally that gets used to document and manage financial operations, processing goods and outputs, and generating reports. It has numerous features that create accounting simple and productive, and makes it easy to use for users.

If you are interested in advancing your accountancy skills and learning how to utilize Tally. Then you need to know about it in detail.

This blog will go through all the things you need to know in order to become an expert.

1. Know and understand the Basics

Just like in any profession, having a solid foundation of basic concepts is essential to improving your Tally skills. For example:

The accounting equation:

This equation states that

assets = liabilities + owners' equity

In Tally, you will find that the company's Balance Sheet follows this equation.

Debits and credits:

Debits are amounts added to your account, while credits are amounts taken away from your account. In Tally, you should know what debits and credits are and what transactions need them like recording a sale or buying purchasing goods.

Different types of accounts:

In Tally, you need to understand how different types of accounts like assets, liabilities, income, and expenses are used to classify your financial transactions.

Learning and becoming proficient in tally obviously is not an easy task but also not an impossible task. With right practice you can become proficient in operating tally. Look for a tally certificate course that will provide you both proficiency and accreditation.

2. Installation and Setup

The first step to using Tally is to download and install the software on your computer.

Once installed, you need to set up your company's details in Tally, such as the

  • company name
  • address
  • contact information
  • financial year

This step ensures that Tally is configured to meet your specific accounting requirements and enables you to start using Tally efficiently.

3. Interaction and Interface

Take some time to explore the Tally interface. Familiarize yourself with the different menus, buttons, and shortcuts. Learn how you can switch between different screens and reports, and how to customize the various pre-defined templates and reports to your needs

For example, if you own a retail store that sells clothes, you can use a variety of marketing tactics, such as: In the event of this, you can design an invoice template in Tally where on the top it shows the customer’s name, the date of sale, the item’s name, the quantity sold, the price per unit, the subtotal and the total amount payable. You can also make modifications to this template and insert your store logo, mode of payment, and other necessary information.

4. Creating and Managing Accounts

In Tally, accounts are like the ingredients in a recipe. You can create different types of accounts like

  • ledgers for tracking your cash
  • bank accounts
  • expenses

As a business owner, you need to understand how to create and manage these accounts effectively.

For example, suppose you run an online store that sells handmade products. In that case, you need to create accounts for expenses like raw materials, packaging, shipping, and marketing costs. You also need to create a bank account ledger to track your cash flow and reconcile your bank statements.

5. Recording Transactions

One of the key functionalities of Tally is that it helps you record and manage financial transactions. You need to understand how to enter transactions accurately and how to adjust any errors that you may make. For example:

  • Recording a sale: Suppose a customer purchases a handmade bracelet from your online store. You need to create a sales voucher in Tally that records the customer's name, the date of sale, the item's name, the quantity sold, the price per item, and the total amount paid.
  • Recording a purchase: Suppose you purchase raw materials like beads and thread to make the bracelet. You need to create a purchase voucher in Tally that records the vendor's name, the date of purchase, the item's name, the quantity purchased, the price per item, and the total amount paid.
